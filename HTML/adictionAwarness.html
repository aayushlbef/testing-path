Addiction Awareness

Understanding substance use, recognizing risks, and accessing support for recovery and prevention.

Understanding Addiction

Addiction is a complex brain disorder characterized by compulsive substance use despite harmful consequences. It's not a moral failing or lack of willpower—it's a medical condition that changes brain chemistry and requires professional treatment.

College students face unique pressures that can increase vulnerability to substance use, including academic stress, social expectations, newfound independence, and mental health challenges. Understanding the risks and recognizing warning signs early can prevent serious problems and save lives.

!

Neglecting responsibilities and academic work

!

Experiencing withdrawal symptoms

!

Increasing tolerance (needing more to feel effects)

!

Failed attempts to cut back or quit

!

Relationship problems due to substance use

!

Risky behaviors while under the influence

!

Spending significant time/money on substances

Prevention & Healthy Choices

Know Your Limits

If you choose to drink, understand safe limits. For most adults, moderate drinking means up to 1 drink per day for women and 2 for men. Binge drinking is 4+ drinks for women or 5+ for men within 2 hours.

Find Alternatives

Develop healthy coping strategies for stress: exercise, meditation, hobbies, social activities, counseling. Join substance-free social groups and events.

Set Boundaries

It's okay to say no to substances. Prepare responses to peer pressure. Surround yourself with supportive friends who respect your choices.

Stay Informed

Educate yourself about substances and their effects. Understand the risks of mixing substances. Know the signs of overdose and how to respond.

Harm Reduction Strategies

If you or someone you know uses substances, these strategies can reduce risks:

Never Use Alone

Always have someone present who can call for help if needed.

Stay Hydrated

Drink water, especially when consuming alcohol.

Don't Mix Substances

Combining substances increases risk of dangerous interactions and overdose.

Know Overdose Signs

Unconsciousness, slow/irregular breathing, blue lips/fingernails—call 1166 immediately.

Have Naloxone Available

For opioid use, keep Narcan (naloxone) accessible—it can reverse overdoses.

Path to Recovery

Recovery is possible, and seeking help is a sign of strength. Treatment options include:

Professional Support

• Individual counseling/therapy

• Group therapy sessions

• Medication-assisted treatment

• Inpatient/outpatient programs

Peer Support

• 12-step programs (AA, NA)

• SMART Recovery meetings

• Campus recovery programs

• Online support communities
